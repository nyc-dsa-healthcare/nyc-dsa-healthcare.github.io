## What is the New York Health Act?

The New York Health Act (NYHA) is legislation that would create a universal, single-payer healthcare system for all of New York State. Every person living or working in New York — regardless of income, employment, or immigration status — would be enrolled automatically and receive comprehensive coverage with no premiums, no deductibles, and no copays.

The NYHA would cover medical, dental, vision, mental health, substance use treatment, and long-term care. You would be free to choose any doctor, hospital, or provider in the state without worrying about networks or prior authorizations. Coverage would never lapse because you lost your job, changed employers, or couldn't afford a premium.

The bill has passed the State Assembly multiple times. The fight to pass it in the State Senate is ongoing — and it is a fight driven by organizing, not by waiting.

## What Does a Democratic Socialist Healthcare System Look Like?

A democratic socialist approach to healthcare starts from a simple premise: healthcare is a human right, not a commodity. That means the goal of the system is to keep people healthy — not to generate profit for insurance companies, pharmaceutical corporations, or hospital executives.

In practice, a democratic socialist healthcare system would be:

**Universally accessible.** No one is turned away. No one skips care because they can't afford it. Coverage is a guarantee of citizenship and residency, not a benefit tied to a job or an income bracket.

**Publicly financed.** The system is funded through progressive taxation — those with more pay more, those with less pay less — rather than through premiums that hit working people the hardest. Administrative costs are slashed because there is one payer, not hundreds of competing insurers.

**Democratically accountable.** Instead of boards of directors answerable to shareholders, a public healthcare system is accountable to the people it serves. Communities have a voice in how care is delivered, where facilities are built, and how resources are distributed.

**Oriented toward prevention.** When care is free at the point of service, people seek treatment early rather than waiting until a condition becomes an emergency. This saves lives and saves money.

Countries with single-payer or similarly structured systems — including Canada, the United Kingdom, and much of Europe — consistently achieve better health outcomes at lower per-capita cost than the United States. The New York Health Act is how we build that here.

## Why Unions Should Care About NYHA — and Take Healthcare Off the Bargaining Table

For decades, unions have fought hard at the bargaining table to win and protect healthcare benefits for their members. That fight has been real and necessary. But it has also come at a cost.

When healthcare is a bargaining chip, it becomes a lever employers use against workers. Management demands concessions on wages, pensions, and working conditions in exchange for maintaining coverage. Strikes get broken because members fear losing their health insurance. Workers stay in jobs they should leave — or avoid strikes they should support — because the alternative is losing coverage for their families.

The New York Health Act changes this calculus entirely. If every New Yorker is covered regardless of employment, healthcare is no longer something workers have to fight for at the table. It is guaranteed. That frees unions to bargain for higher wages, better hours, safer conditions, and more control over the workplace — without having to trade those gains away to keep their members insured.

There is also a direct financial benefit. Employers currently spend significant portions of their labor costs on health benefits. Under the NYHA, those costs are replaced by a payroll contribution to the public system — typically lower than what employers pay now. That money can be redirected to wages.

Universal healthcare is a labor issue. The New York Health Act is a union issue. Taking healthcare off the bargaining table does not weaken workers — it strengthens them.
